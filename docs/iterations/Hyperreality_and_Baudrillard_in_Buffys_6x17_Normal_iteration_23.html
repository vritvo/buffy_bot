Detailed Comments

This paper attempts to resolve 'Normal Again's' deliberate ambiguity by arguing that the episode provides clear evidence favoring the Sunnydale reality, but this interpretation cannot be sustained against the actual script. The episode's final scene explicitly shows the asylum continuing to exist after Buffy makes her choice, with the doctor declaring 'I'm afraid we lost her' as Joyce sobs - hardly evidence of a 'clear resolution' favoring one reality over another. The paper's claim that the demon's poison provides a 'pharmacological explanation' ignores that this explanation exists only within the Sunnydale reality and could equally be part of Buffy's delusion. While the paper offers valuable insights about the episode's treatment of trauma and agency, its fundamental misreading of the text undermines its scholarly credibility. The analysis of the producer disagreement is well-handled, but the paper's insistence on resolving the episode's intentional ambiguity contradicts both Whedon's explicit statements about interpretive flexibility and the episode's actual structure.

The script explicitly establishes the asylum as a demon-induced hallucination through medical framework, timing of visions, and narrative structure. The paper's claim that the episode maintains genuine ambiguity contradicts the textual evidence. The analysis of the Whedon-Noxon disagreement is insightful, but the paper overstates this as evidence of the episode's philosophical sophistication rather than recognizing it as a disagreement about interpretation rather than authorial intent.
