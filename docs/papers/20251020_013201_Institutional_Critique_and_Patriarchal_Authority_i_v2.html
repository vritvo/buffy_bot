Institutional Critique and Patriarchal Authority in 4x10 'Hush': Silence as Resistance

Abstract

This paper analyzes Buffy the Vampire Slayer's "Hush" (4.10) as a sophisticated critique of patriarchal authority that reveals how institutional power depends on controlling discourse while authentic communication transcends linguistic structures entirely. Through the episode's central conceit of enforced silence, the Gentlemen emerge as a distilled representation of patriarchal institutional power, operating through systematic silencing, hierarchical violence, and performative politeness that masks brutality. When stripped of verbal communication, characters discover forms of authentic connection that bypass traditional power structures, most notably in the introduction of Willow and Tara's relationship, which develops through magical rather than verbal intimacy. The episode's technical innovation of minimal dialogue serves not merely as formal experimentation but as thematic embodiment of its central argument: that meaningful resistance to patriarchal authority occurs through authentic expression that exists beyond approved institutional discourse. Buffy's climactic scream represents not traditional feminine victimization but the reclamation of voice as active resistance, positioning authentic communication as the primary weapon against systems that depend on controlling who can speak, when, and how.
